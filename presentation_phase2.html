Where Need Meets Silence

Why do some Israeli communities receive far fewer disability benefits than their conditions would predict?

TovTech Research Group  |  National Insurance Institute data, Dec 2024  |  278 settlements  |  4 research questions

People Who Qualify for Disability Benefits Are Not Receiving Them

Israel’s National Insurance Institute pays disability benefits to hundreds of thousands of people. But in some settlements, far fewer people claim than we would expect given their socio-economic conditions. We analyzed 278 settlements with 15 indicators to find out where — and why.

What we did

We trained machine learning models to predict disability benefit rates from socio-economic data (income, education, demographics, geography). Settlements where actual claiming is far below the prediction are potential under-utilization pockets — places where eligible people may face barriers to access.

What the Model Uses & What It Found

To explain the general disability benefit rate across settlements, I used eight standardised explanatory variables: log salary, peripherality index, income support rate, share of population aged 65+, labour participation rate, dependency ratio, and two dummy variables — Arab-majority settlement and Haredi-majority settlement (OLS with HC3 robust standard errors; N = 263, R² = 0.699).

Five of these variables are statistically significant (p < 0.05): log salary (β = −1.89, p < 0.001), Arab-majority settlement (β = −1.41, p = 0.004), share 65+ (β = +0.98, p < 0.001), dependency ratio (β = −0.66, p < 0.001), and peripherality index (β = −0.26, p = 0.005). In plain terms: a settlement with an Arab majority is associated with a 1.41 percentage-point lower disability rate, all else equal; a one-standard-deviation increase in the share of residents aged 65 and above is associated with a 0.98 pp higher disability-benefit rate on average; and a one-SD increase in log salary is associated with a 1.89 pp lower disability rate.

The Pattern That Shouldn’t Exist

In non-peripheral settlements (r = 0.696), the child-adult disability link is stronger than in the periphery (r = 0.523). The difference is statistically significant (Fisher’s Z = -2.183, p < 0.05). Wealth alone does not break the cycle.

Overall Picture

High-Risk settlements combine: low socio-economic standing (-0.36 vs -0.12), lower wages (₪11,169 vs ₪12,278), higher income support dependence, weaker labor market participation, and lower education — creating a self-reinforcing environment where both adults and children depend on disability benefits. 5 of 6 indicators show statistically significant differences (Welch’s T-test). Targeted holistic family rehabilitation programs are needed.

What This Means for Policy

The 32 high-risk settlements need holistic family-level rehabilitation programs, not individual disability support alone. Breaking the intergenerational cycle requires simultaneous intervention in education, employment, and healthcare access.

We Removed the Arab Variable — Nothing Changed

We re-ran the ensemble without arab_population_percentage. The under-utilization list stayed identical. The model doesn’t need an “Arab” label — it reconstructs the group from the remaining 14 indicators: low SES, low salary, zero Haredi %, specific peripherality and education patterns. No single variable carries the signal; it is distributed across the entire socio-economic profile.

This means the model normalizes the under-claiming pattern. It learns “settlements that look like this tend to claim less” and adjusts its prediction downward — turning systemic access barriers into a statistical baseline. The discrimination is not in one column; it is woven into the structure of the data.

This Has Happened Before

Obermeyer et al. (2019, Science): A US healthcare algorithm trained on 200 million patients used cost as proxy for need. Because the system spent less on Black patients with equivalent illness, the algorithm concluded they were healthier.

Our case is analogous: NII data records benefit receipt as proxy for disability prevalence. In communities facing access barriers, fewer people claim — creating the illusion of lower need.

Convergence of Evidence

Our data, the qualitative research, and the international precedent all point to the same conclusion:

  • The R² wall (~82% unexplained) = unmeasured barriers
  • The under-utilization clusters (6% Arab-majority) = families not claiming despite need
  • The hidden burden clusters (36% Arab-majority) = mostly non-Arab; the Arab gap is about access, not over-diagnosis
  • The intergenerational pattern = access failure starts in childhood

Break the Intergenerational Cycle

From Q2: settlements flagged for both child and adult disability (ρ = 0.461) need integrated family-level interventions, not separate programs. The pattern persists even in affluent areas (ρ = 0.715 in the center).

Annual Monitoring Dashboard

From Q3: re-run the model annually. Settlements with persistent positive gaps across multiple years should be prioritized for field investigation. Track frontline communities (4 settlements) for post-conflict effects.
