What This Means

Settlements where more children receive disability benefits also have higher adult disability rates. This suggests a cycle of vulnerability: conditions that lead to childhood disability persist into adulthood.

Stronger in the Center

Paradoxically, the center shows a stronger correlation (ρ = 0.715) than the periphery (ρ = 0.53). Even in affluent areas, intergenerational disability patterns persist, suggesting structural rather than purely economic causes.

Q1 Signal: Under-Utilization Is Concentrated Here

Of the 28 flagged settlements, 36% are Arab-majority. The model predicts high disability rates, but observed claiming is far below prediction. Standard factors (income, education, demographics) explain R² = 0.798 for secular settlements but almost nothing for Arab ones.

Q2 Signal: The Intergenerational Pattern Is Present

The child-adult disability correlation exists across all sectors, but in Arab communities, the model residual is largest — meaning the gap between predicted and actual is not random. Something invisible to official statistics drives the gap, and Q2 shows this pattern starts in childhood. This convergence points to access barriers, not lower need.

The Remaining Candidates

If not physical distance, then what?

  • Informational: people don't know they're eligible
  • Linguistic: forms and committees operate in Hebrew
  • Cultural: stigma prevents claiming
  • Institutional: the process itself deters applicants

Israeli Research Confirms What Our Models Show

Two independent 2024 studies validate the access-barrier hypothesis

Alhuzeel et al. (2024) — Scandinavian J. of Disability Research

“It’s Disgraceful Going through All this for Being an Arab and Disabled”

Interviews with 15 Arab Israelis with disabilities revealed barriers at every level:

  • Family: disability perceived as shameful; families avoid state services to prevent reduced marriage prospects
  • Institution: NII perceived as the most discriminatory government body. Medical committees lack Arabic-speaking staff
  • Society: “the invisible of the invisible” — Arab disabled people face compounded marginalization

Brookdale Institute (2024) — People with Disabilities in the Arab Population

Arab self-reported disability prevalence is higher than Jewish — 21% vs. 19% — yet benefit claiming is lower.

Arab people with disabilities face “double exclusion”. Women face “triple exclusion” (minority + disability + gender).

35% of people already approved for benefits never exercised eligibility (Brookdale, 2022). 23% were simply unaware of the approval.

The Science Parallel: Obermeyer et al. (2019)

Science, 366(6464): A US healthcare algorithm trained on 200 million patients used cost as proxy for need. Because the system spent less on Black patients with equivalent illness, the algorithm concluded they were healthier.

Our case is analogous: NII data records benefit receipt as proxy for disability prevalence. In communities facing access barriers, fewer people claim — creating the illusion of lower need. Our R² wall for Arab settlements (0.41) is statistical evidence of this same pattern.

Convergence of Evidence

Our data-driven finding — that standard models fail for Arab settlements — aligns precisely with the qualitative research:

  • The R² wall (models explain ~59% less for Arab settlements) = unmeasured barriers
  • The under-utilization clusters (36% are Arab-majority) = families not claiming despite need
  • The distance non-effect (slope -0.82) = the barrier is not physical but informational, linguistic, and institutional

Recommendations

Evidence-based actions to close the disability benefit access gap

1

Targeted Outreach

The 28 flagged settlements should receive proactive information campaigns — in Arabic, with community-based intermediaries and local advocacy organizations.

2

Simplify the Process

Bhargava & Manoli (2015): simplifying benefit language increased claiming by 6-8 pp. Show estimated benefit amounts, use plain language, reduce paperwork.

3

Arabic-Language Services

Ensure NII medical committees include Arabic-speaking professionals. Provide all forms, notifications, and digital services in Arabic.

4

Break the Intergenerational Cycle

Settlements flagged for both child and adult disability (Q2) need integrated family-level interventions, not separate programs.

5

Annual Monitoring

Re-run the model annually. Settlements with persistent positive gaps across multiple years should be prioritized for field investigation. Track frontline communities for post-conflict effects.

Summary: Four questions, one conclusion: disability benefit under-utilization in Israel is concentrated, intergenerational, structural, and invisible to standard data. Our models explain ~75% of the variation overall (R² = 0.75) but hit a wall for Arab settlements (R² = 0.41). This is evidence that the factors driving the gap — language barriers, institutional friction, and systemic under-investment — are invisible to official statistics.
